About

What is KinderMiner?

KinderMiner is a simple text mining method for ranking a set of target terms by association with a key phrase. For example, KinderMiner can be used to rank transcription factors (the target terms) by their association with embryonic stem cells (the key phrase), in order to find what transcription factors are important to the embryonic stem cell state.

How does KinderMiner work?

KinerMiner counts the number of articles (available on Europe PubMed Central) in which each term occurs with the key phrase, in which each term occurs without the key phrase, and in which the key phrase occurs without the terms. It uses these counts to perform a simple statistical calculation to filter and rank the terms by their co-occurrence with the key phrase. In this way, co-occurrence is assumed to represent association between any one term and the key phrase.

How do I use this KinderMiner tool?

  1. Enter your Email address. This is where your results will be sent when KinderMiner completes your request. Note: A query may take several hours to complete.
  2. Enter a Key Phrase for the topic you want to rank on. This is the general application you're interested in. For example, if you want to find transcription factors associated with embryonic stem cells, 'embryonic stem cell' (without quotes) would be your Key Phrase.
  3. Enter a list of Target Terms (one per line) to rank by their association with the Key Phrase above. For example, you might enter a complete list of transcription factors to be ranked by association with 'embryonic stem cell' or another cell type. Note: We recommend using one of our quick fill options below the box.
  4. (optional) Enter a Censor Year if you would like KinderMiner to only count articles published through a particular year. Note: We recommend leaving it at the current year by default.
  5. (optional) Enter an FET Cutoff to specify a different Fisher Exact Test p-value to filter target terms in the results. Note: We recommend leaving it at the default of 1e-5. You may wish to make this smaller if you have too many terms in your final results, or larger if you have too few.
